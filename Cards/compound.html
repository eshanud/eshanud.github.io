About the Project
A smarter way to think about money

Many financial apps treat expense tracking, savings goals, and reminders as separate flows. Compound explores what happens when these decisions are brought into one connected experience. When you see progress toward a goal, every transaction becomes a decision. not just a log entry.

Designed end-to-end: from onboarding and authentication through daily tracking, goal-setting, reminders, and support chat.

Mobile App Fintech UX Design 2024–2025
Research

What users actually needed

5 user conversations and a review of 4 competitor apps revealed a consistent frustration. not with budgeting, but with fragmentation.

5

Core insight
Users couldn't connect spending habits with savings goals. The tools were always separate. so the meaning was lost.
Key pain points identified
01. Logging
"I track everything but never look back at it."

Expenses were logged but never reviewed. the habit existed without the insight.

02. Goals
"I set a savings goal and forgot about it in a week."

Goals lived in a separate app. invisible during daily spending decisions.

03. Reminders
"My bills are in my calendar, my loans in notes, my subscriptions nowhere."

Payment reminders were scattered across 3–4 different places.

04. The ask
"I just want one place that tells me where I stand."

Users wanted spending, goals, and bills in one view. not three open tabs.

  • →Expense apps don't connect to goals. spending is logged but its impact on savings is never shown.
  • →Goals stay invisible during daily use. they live in a separate tab, out of sight when they matter most.
  • →Reminders are scattered. bills, loans, and savings deposits tracked across three different places.

Iteration. goal placement

The most significant structural change during the design process.

Before
Goals buried in a "Savings" tab
Initial layout placed the goal list as a separate bottom-nav tab. Users had to navigate away from the dashboard to check progress. replicating the exact fragmentation we were solving.
Problem: goals stayed invisible during the logging moment
→
After
Active goal summary on dashboard
Moved a compact goal-progress card directly onto the dashboard, above the transaction list. The goal becomes ambient. always visible when logging a new expense, creating passive accountability.
Rationale: zero extra taps to see progress

Key design decisions

The decisions that shaped the structure. and what was traded off to get there.

Decision 01
Reminders are unified. not split into bills vs. goals

A common pattern is separate "Bills" and "Savings" reminder lists. Compound merges them intentionally. a loan payment and a savings deposit are both recurring financial commitments and deserve the same mental weight.

Decision 02
Progress bars over percentages only

Showing only "50%" feels abstract. A filled progress bar on a goal card creates a spatial sense of distance. you can see how close or far you are at a glance, without calculating anything.

Solution

Three tools. One system.

Compound brings expense tracking, savings goals, and reminders together. Every rupee spent connects to what you're saving for.

Expenses and goals aren't separate. They're two sides of the same decision.

How Compound compares

Apps like YNAB and Monarch do parts of this well. Compound's bet is on unification. not depth in any one area.

Validation

Testing plan

Three tasks to validate the core flows. each with a clear pass/fail.

01
Set up a savings goal for a real target
Success metric
Completed without help in under 2 minutes
Fail state
User asks "where do I add a goal?" or exits flow
02
Log an expense and identify how it affects a goal
Success metric
User references the goal card unprompted within 30 seconds of logging
Fail state
User logs expense without noticing goal progress update
03
Set a monthly bill reminder from scratch
Success metric
Zero navigation errors, reminder appears in list within 90 seconds
Fail state
User looks for reminders in the wrong section or backs out

Accessibility

Built for everyone

Color independence

Green and red are never the sole signal. Every amount carries a +/− symbol and primary actions meet WCAG AA contrast.

Overdue visibility

Missed payments surface with a red "Due" badge on the dashboard. No tab-switching needed to stay aware.

Touch-first sizing

All interactive elements meet 44×44pt. Progress bars show percentage text so they inform without triggering action.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

What worked

Keeping every decision anchored to one insight. fragmentation. stopped scope creep. Nothing was added for aesthetics. Every screen had to earn its place.

The honest gap

The goal card on the dashboard is the core hypothesis. seeing a savings target should make users pause before spending. That assumption was never tested. It's the one thing I'd validate first next time.
